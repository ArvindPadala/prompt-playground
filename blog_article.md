Does the model get more creative, or just more chaotic?* Does Mistral handle sarcasm differently from Gemma? At what point does "randomness" become "hallucination"?

I built the **Prompt Engineering Playground** to answer these questions. It runs entirely on my laptop. It costs nothing per query. It puts Mistral, Gemma, and Llama2 in the same room and lets you ask them the same question at the same time.

Here's everything you need to know — about the project, the concepts under the hood, and why this stuff matters more in March 2026 than it ever has.

## The Problem With Treating LLMs Like Vending Machines

Most people interact with AI the way they interact with a vending machine: put something in, get something out, hope the output matches expectations. If it doesn't — rephrase vaguely and try again.

That works until it doesn't. When you're building a product, writing a paper, debugging a model's weird behaviour, or just trying to understand *why* one prompt gets a sharp answer and a near-identical one gets nonsense — vending machine thinking fails you.

The Prompt Engineering Playground is an antidote. It's built on a simple belief: **the best way to understand a system is to vary one thing at a time and watch what changes.** It's the scientific method applied to language models. And it runs on your laptop, privately, for free.

---

## What the Project Actually Does

At its core the app is a Streamlit web interface that connects to [Ollama](https://ollama.ai/) — a blazing-fast local LLM runtime — and lets you:

- **Write a prompt** (and optionally a system/instruction prompt)
- **Pick one or more models** — Mistral, Gemma, Llama2 — from sidebar checkboxes
- **Tune decoding parameters** — temperature, top-p, top-k — with real-time sliders
- **Watch responses stream live**, token by token, as they're generated
- **Compare models side by side** in a full-width list layout
- **Track token stats** — how many tokens went in, how many came out, how fast

Select all three models and every response runs **in parallel** (via Python's `ThreadPoolExecutor`), so you're not waiting serially. Select one and you get the ChatGPT-style live typewriter effect.

Everything runs locally. Prompts never leave your machine.

## The Concepts That Make It Tick

### Tokens: The Currency of LLMs

Here's something that surprises most people: language models don't read words. They read **tokens** — sub-word units generated by an algorithm called Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE), introduced by [Sennrich et al. in 2016](https://arxiv.org/abs/1508.07909).

`"unhappiness"` is two tokens: `"un"` + `"happiness"`. `"ChatGPT"` is three: `"Chat"`, `"G"`, `"PT"`. `"a"` is one.

Why does this matter? Because the token count determines **cost, speed, and context capacity**. The playground shows you prompt tokens and output tokens after every generation — you start to develop an intuition for which phrasings are more "expensive" and whether verbosity in your prompt buys you better outputs.

---

### Temperature: Not "Creativity", Actually Entropy

Everyone's heard "turn up the temperature for more creative responses." That's a passable intuition. Here's the accurate one.

When an LLM is about to generate the next token, it produces a **probability distribution** over its entire vocabulary (30,000–100,000 words). Temperature is a divisor applied to those scores before the distribution is computed:

```
P(token) = exp(score / temperature) / sum(exp(all scores / temperature))
```

At **temperature = 0.1**, the distribution becomes a spike. The model almost always picks the single highest-probability token — deterministic, precise, sometimes boring.

At **temperature = 1.5**, the distribution flattens. Tokens with low probabilities get promoted. The model takes detours. Beautiful, sometimes. Incoherent, often.

The insight: what we call "creativity" is statistically speaking just **entropy injection into a learned distribution**. You're not making the model more imaginative — you're making it less sure.

> **2024 research check:** A study presented at [EMNLP 2024](https://aclanthology.org/) found that temperature changes between 0.0 and 1.0 have *no statistically significant impact on problem-solving performance* across multiple LLMs. The model's training distribution dominates. Beyond 1.5, quality degrades as format inconsistencies emerge. Your mental model of "temperature = creativity dial" needs to be replaced with "temperature = precision/diversity tradeoff, with diminishing returns."

---

### Top-k and Top-p: The Gatekeepers

At every generation step, the model has access to every token in its vocabulary. Top-k and top-p are filters that restrict which tokens can be sampled from.

**Top-k** keeps the `k` most probable tokens and discards the rest. Simple, effective, but brittle — a fixed `k=50` applies the same constraint whether the model is highly confident (peaked distribution) or genuinely uncertain (flat distribution).

**Top-p** (nucleus sampling, from [Holtzman et al. 2020](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.09751)) is smarter. It keeps the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability exceeds `p`. When the model is confident, the nucleus is small. When uncertain, it expands. The filter adapts.

A creative intuition: think of top-k as a fixed spotlight and top-p as a spotlight that grows brighter in the dark.

> **Emerging research:** A new method called **Min-P sampling** ([ArXiv 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.01082)) is gaining traction in open-source deployments. Instead of a fixed threshold, Min-P scales the cutoff relative to the highest-probability token — dynamically raising the bar based on the model's own confidence. Several open-source frameworks have already adopted it. The playground uses top-k and top-p today; adding Min-P would be a meaningful next step.

### Prompting: The Discipline Hidden in Plain Sight

Prompt engineering is often dismissed as "just writing better questions." That's like calling architecture "just drawing buildings."

The prompt is the *only* lever you have over an already-trained model without touching its weights. How you structure that lever determines everything: the format of the response, the depth of reasoning, the persona the model adopts, whether it hallucinates or cites.

The system prompt field in the playground makes this visceral. Change it from:
- *(empty)* → get a generic assistant
- `"You respond only in haiku"` → every output becomes 5-7-5
- `"You are a skeptical peer reviewer who assumes every claim is wrong until proven"` → adversarial, rigorous responses

This isn't a party trick. It's how every commercial AI product works. OpenAI's system prompt for ChatGPT Plus runs to hundreds of lines. Every enterprise chatbot is a raw model steered by a carefully engineered instruction layer.

> **Industry signal:** [Gartner](https://www.gartner.com/) forecasts that 70% of enterprises will deploy AI-driven *automated* prompt optimisation by 2026 — systems that improve their own prompts. Structured prompt processes have been shown to [reduce AI errors by up to 76%](https://sqmagazine.co.uk/). The playground is a manual laboratory for building the intuitions that underpin those automated systems.

### Streaming: You're Watching the Model Think

The typewriter effect isn't cosmetic. It reflects the actual computation order.

LLMs generate **one token at a time**, in a loop. Each new token is conditioned on every token before it. The model can't look ahead. It can't revise. It commits, then moves on.

When you watch tokens appear in real time, you're watching autoregressive inference happen. When the output takes a strange turn halfway through, you're watching the model make a locally reasonable choice that becomes globally incoherent because it can't backtrack.

Under the hood, Ollama returns [NDJSON](http://ndjson.org/) — one JSON object per token. The playground reads each line as it arrives and passes it through a Python generator into Streamlit's `st.write_stream()`. No buffering, no waiting. First token appears within milliseconds of generation starting.

### Local LLMs: The Actually Important Part

Everything described above runs on a laptop. No cloud. No API key. No data leaving the machine.

This isn't a novelty — it's a structural shift. In 2022, running a 7B-parameter model required a high-end GPU server. In 2026, [Ollama](https://ollama.ai/) handles it on a MacBook Air with 16GB RAM, using Apple's Metal GPU and 4-bit quantised GGUF model weights.

The implications are significant:
- **Healthcare and legal teams** can use AI on sensitive documents without cloud exposure
- **Developers in countries with data localisation laws** can build AI products that were previously impossible
- **Students and researchers** access state-of-the-art models at zero marginal cost

> Ollama is now described as "[the default choice for local LLMs by 2026](https://dev.to/)," with multimodal support, a native desktop GUI, and enterprise deployments on AWS and Alibaba Cloud — ironic for a "local" tool, but it speaks to its API compatibility and versatility.

```
         │
         ▼
   Streamlit App (app.py)
         │
    ┌────┴────┐
    │         │
    ▼         ▼  (parallel threads)
models/     models/
mistral  +  gemma  +  llama
         │
         ▼
  Ollama REST API (:11434)
         │
    ┌────┼────┐
    ▼    ▼    ▼
 Mistral Gemma Llama2
  GGUF  GGUF   GGUF
    (on your GPU/CPU)
```

Every API call uses `stream=True`. Responses flow through Python generators. Multi-model runs use `ThreadPoolExecutor`. Token stats come from the final JSON chunk. The whole thing is ~440 lines of Python.

## What You Could Build Next

The playground is a foundation that's deliberately easy to extend. Here are five directions worth exploring:

**1. Swap in newer models.** Change `"llama2"` to `"llama3.1"` in [models/llama_model.py](file:///Users/arvindpadala/Projects/prompt-playground/models/llama_model.py) — which you've already pulled locally. The entire infrastructure stays the same. You immediately have an 8B-parameter model with dramatically better reasoning.

**2. Add Min-P sampling.** Ollama supports custom sampling parameters. Adding a Min-P slider would make the playground a research tool for comparing sampling strategies directly — something even most professional ML teams don't have.

**3. Export and compare results.** Capture all three model outputs + parameters into a JSON file per session. Run 20 variations on the same prompt. Now you have a dataset. You can quantitatively measure which parameter combinations produce which styles.

**4. RAG integration.** Connect a local document store (e.g., a PDF via `pypdf`, chunked and embedded with `nomic-embed-text` on Ollama) to the prompt. Now you can ask questions about your own documents — a private, local RAG system in under 100 extra lines.

**5. Fine-tuning feedback loop.** Record which outputs you rate highly. Export those (prompt, response, rating) pairs. Use that data to fine-tune the model on your preferred style with [Unsloth](https://github.com/unslothai/unsloth) or similar. The playground becomes the data collection layer for personalised model training.

## Why This, Why Now

The AI field has a funny paradox: the more powerful the tools become, the more people use them without understanding them. You get practitioners who can prompt GPT-4 but couldn't explain why their prompt works. You get engineers who deploy LLMs into production but treat decoding parameters as knobs to fiddle randomly.

The Prompt Engineering Playground is a corrective. It insists on transparency — you see the tokens, see the stats, see the parameters, see three models respond to the same input side by side. It runs locally, so there's no abstraction between you and the model.

The concepts it teaches — autoregressive generation, decoding strategies, prompt design, local inference — aren't going anywhere. Models will get larger and faster. The parameter names may change. But the fundamental mechanics of how you communicate with and control a language model are the bedrock skill of AI engineering in 2026.

Build the playground. Run it. Change the temperature and watch what breaks. Pit Mistral against Gemma on a problem you care about. Type a system prompt that makes Llama2 respond like a pirate. Then figure out why it works.

That's how intuition becomes expertise.
